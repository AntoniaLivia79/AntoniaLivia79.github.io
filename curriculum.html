A Personal Learning Curriculum

The Witching
Scriptorium

A Year of Deep Reading, Wonder & the Written Word

This is a living curriculum — a doorway into a world you are building as much as discovering.
Each book is a stone in the path. Each journal entry is a spell cast in ink.
You are both scholar and wanderer. Both keeper and seeker.

I. The Philosophy

The Witching Scriptorium is structured as a year-long journey divided into four seasonal chapters, each with its own character, its own books, and its own imaginative world to inhabit. Within each season you spend roughly five hours a week: some in reading, some in writing, some simply sitting with a thought.

Your paper journal is not a passive record. It is a grimoire of learning — a place where research and imagination meet. Each season asks you to build a small inner world: to populate it, name it, walk through it, and let what you are reading deepen it. By year's end, you will have four distinct worlds in your journal, and a year's worth of genuine scholarship woven through them.

· · ·

II. The Three Pillars

Every season rests on three equal pillars. All activities connect back to one or more of these.

📖 The Lore

Reading & scholarship. Research notes, timelines, source comparisons, annotated maps.

🌿 The Land

The English landscape, cathedrals, nature walks. Field notes, sketches, observations brought home in ink.

✍️ The World

Creative imagining. Your own invented world, grown richer with each season, fed by what you read.

· · ·

III. The Goals

Scholarship

Read 8 books across the year (2 per season), alternating fiction and non-fiction, with genuine depth of engagement — not just plot, but idea.

Creative Practice

Complete all seasonal journal exercises. By December, possess a handwritten world with its own geography, mythology, figures and lore.

Connection

Make at least one real-world outing per season: a cathedral, a forest, a stone circle. Bring something back in writing.

Synthesis

At each season's close, write a short personal essay (2–3 journal pages) connecting what you have read, made, and experienced.

· · ·

IV. The Weekly Rhythm

Across five hours a week, a gentle structure to work with rather than against.

Reading
Two or three sessions of sustained reading — at least one by candlelight or firelight if the season allows.
The Lore
One journal session for research notes, marginalia, mind-maps, timelines or annotated entries from reading.
The World
One journal session for the creative exercise of the season — building your world, writing a fragment, inhabiting a character.
The Land
One walk, outing or moment of deliberate observation. Even a cathedral close or a garden counts. Bring it home in a single journal paragraph.

V. The Four Seasons

🌱

Season I — Spring: The Waking World

Samhain to Imbolc in spirit; the world before the veil is fully drawn back
Themes: Origins · Sacred Landscape · The Other Beneath the Surface

This season asks you to begin. You are digging into the deep soil of English sacred landscape — the pre-Christian layer, the fairy-haunted hollows, the standing stones. You are also building the map of your invented world: its geography, its hidden places, its sacred sites.

The Books

  • The Ancient Ways: A Pagan Guide to Sustainable Living — Nigel Pennick Non-fiction
    Pennick is already known to you via Eight Winds — this deepens the landscape layer. Focus on the chapters concerning sacred sites and the genius loci.
  • The Once and Future King — T.H. White Fiction
    A counterpoint to your existing Arthurian reading — White's Merlin and the book's deep, elegiac Englishness will feel entirely different to academic sources. Pay close attention to landscape, magic and female figures such as Morgan le Fay.
📓 Journal Exercises — The World-Forge
  1. Draw the map. Spend your first creative session drawing (however roughly) the map of your invented world. Give it coasts and marshes, a cathedral city, a forest that is older than the kingdom. Name nothing yet — this comes later.
  2. The Genius Loci. After reading Pennick, choose one real place you know — a cathedral, a wood, a particular hill — and write a full journal page as if describing its spirit to someone who has never been there. What does it want? What does it remember?
  3. The Hidden Place. In your invented world, there is a place beneath a place — an island within an island, a cave beneath the cathedral. Write one page describing it in the voice of someone who has been there and come back changed.
  4. White's Women. After reading The Once and Future King, write a brief character portrait (half a page) of Morgan le Fay as White presents her. Then write a second portrait as you believe she truly is — drawing on your wider reading of the nine sisters tradition.
☀️

Season II — Summer: The Long Light

When the world is loudest and most present — go deeper into myth
Themes: Mythology · The Divine Feminine · The Hero Who Is Not A Man

Summer turns the gaze outward and upward. This season you move into comparative mythology — the great structural patterns beneath folklore — and into fiction that explicitly subverts the expected heroic arc. Your invented world gains its gods, its myths, its founding stories.

The Books

  • The Myth of the Goddess: Evolution of an Image — Anne Baring & Jules Cashford Non-fiction
    A sweeping, serious and beautifully illustrated study of the divine feminine from Palaeolithic Europe to the present. Long but rewards slow, non-linear reading. Treat it as a reference as much as a cover-to-cover text — choose two or three chapters per week that call to you.
  • The Buried Giant — Kazuo Ishiguro Fiction
    Post-Arthurian England, a mist of forgetting, an ageing couple on a strange quest. Quiet, devastating, and soaked in the landscape you love. Connects directly to your interest in the Arthurian cycle and to the English countryside.
📓 Journal Exercises — The Myth-Weaving
  1. The Creation Myth. Write the creation myth of your invented world. One page only. It should contain a goddess, something sacrificed, and something hidden in the earth.
  2. The Forgetting. Ishiguro's central image is collective amnesia — things buried so they cannot wound. Write a journal entry about something your invented world has chosen to forget, and why. What would happen if someone remembered?
  3. A Goddess of Your Making. Drawing on Baring and Cashford, design a goddess for your world. Give her domain, her symbol, her sacred animal, the month she is strongest, and the one thing she cannot forgive. Illustrate or annotate as feels natural.
  4. Field note. Visit a medieval church or cathedral this season. Specifically look for pre-Christian elements — a Green Man, a sheela-na-gig, a suspiciously well-worn saint's statue. Write a single page on what you find and what it tells you about the layering of belief.
🍂

Season III — Autumn: The Thinning

The veil thins. Scholarship turns towards folklore and the uncanny
Themes: Folk Magic · The Liminal · Tradition & Transgression

This is the season most native to your practice. Autumn asks you to go fully into folk belief, the cunning craft, and the strange persistence of old magic in everyday English life. Your invented world gains its folk traditions, its hedge witches, its unspoken rules — the things the ordinary people know.

The Books

  • Cunning Folk and Familiar Spirits — Emma Wilby Non-fiction
    A meticulous and gripping academic study of shamanic visionary traditions in early modern England, specifically the cunning folk and their familiar spirits. Will genuinely surprise and deepen your understanding of English witchcraft's roots. Highly complementary to your existing reading.
  • The Owl Service — Alan Garner Fiction
    Welsh mythology coiled inside a contemporary story, with myth pattern repeating across generations. Short but profound. Garner uses landscape exactly as Moore uses London — as a living palimpsest. A natural companion to Jerusalem and to your Arthurian interests.
📓 Journal Exercises — The Cunning Craft
  1. The Cunning Person of Your World. Draw on Wilby to create a cunning woman or man in your invented world. Write their method: how they are consulted, what their familiar is, what they are willing to do and what they refuse. Make them morally ambiguous.
  2. The Pattern Repeating. Garner's whole project is myth recursion — the same story forcing itself through different people. Identify a myth or folklore pattern you have encountered this year (from any reading) and write it as if it were happening now, in England, to ordinary people.
  3. A Folk Tradition. Invent a single folk tradition for your world — a festival, a prohibition, a charm worked at a crossroads. Write it as if from a folklore compendium: its name, occasion, practice, and the story people tell about why it began.
  4. Autumn walk. A single nature walk this season, specifically looking for evidence of the thinning — fungi, dying light, altered birdsong. Write one page in the voice of a medieval peasant walking the same path.
❄️

Season IV — Winter: The Deep Archive

Candlelight, long evenings, and the questions that remain
Themes: Time · Memory · The Self Across History

Winter turns inward. This final season is about synthesis — bringing together the year's threads, deepening the invented world into something that can hold the weight of all four seasons, and asking the larger questions. The reading here is deliberately more philosophical. Your world receives its history, its wounds, its relationship with time.

The Books

  • The Timeless Way of Building — Christopher Alexander Non-fiction
    A surprising choice — but one that speaks directly to world-building, cathedrals, and the feel of sacred and lived space. Alexander's central argument (that great places have a quality that cannot be named, only recognised) will give your invented world a new architectural and spatial dimension.
  • The Left Hand of Darkness — Ursula K. Le Guin Fiction
    Le Guin is the presiding spirit of serious world-building fiction. Her invented anthropology, her treatment of gender, her mythological interpolations and her winter landscape make this essential. It will reshape how you think about what a world needs to feel real.
📓 Journal Exercises — The Final Weaving
  1. The Cathedral. Using Alexander's ideas about living structure, describe the most important building in your invented world — its proportions, its light, why people feel differently inside it. What does it tell you about what your world values?
  2. The History Page. Write one page of your world's history as if from an actual medieval chronicle — formal Latin-adjacent prose, the dry recording of extraordinary events. Choose a moment of rupture: a plague, a conquest, a miracle.
  3. The Year in Review. Write a three-page personal essay — your voice, your reflections — on the year of reading. What surprised you? What deepened? What has changed in how you understand the landscape around you, your practice, or your sense of time?
  4. The Name. Return to your map from Season I. Name everything that was unnamed. Your world is complete.

VI. The Journal as Grimoire

On Beginning Each Journal Session

Before you write, take one breath and ask yourself which pillar you are serving today — The Lore, The Land, or The World. Write it in the margin. Over time you will see which pillar you are drawn to and which you are quietly avoiding. Both are useful knowledge.

Some practical suggestions for the physical journal. Use a journal large enough to accommodate a small hand-drawn map (A5 at minimum, A4 is ideal). Dedicate the first few pages to a contents record you build as you go — title each entry briefly. Use a single symbol in the margin to mark entries belonging to each pillar: a leaf 🌿 for The Land, a rune or sigil of your choice for The Lore, and a small star or pentagram for The World. By mid-year this will make the journal navigable and beautiful.

Do not worry about beautiful handwriting or polished prose in your research notes. The creative exercises are where voice and care matter. The Lore sections can be as rough as a scholar's working notebook — that is what they are.

VII. Further Reading — The Reserve Shelf

These books are not assigned to any season. They are for when a thread pulls you somewhere else, or when you finish a season's books early. Keep this list at the back of your journal.

  • The Gospel of Loki — Joanne M. Harris Fiction — Norse myth retold from the trickster's perspective; pairs well with comparative mythology work.
  • Witches, Werewolves and Fairies — Claude Lecouteux Non-fiction — Medieval European beliefs about shapeshifting and the double; scholarly and strange.
  • The Green Man — Kathleen Basford Non-fiction — The definitive visual study; beautiful for cathedral visits.
  • Piranesi — Susanna Clarke Fiction — An invented world of uncanny architecture that rewards world-builders enormously.
  • The History of the Kings of Britain — Geoffrey of Monmouth Primary Source — The ur-text of Arthurian legend; even a few chapters read in translation is to drink from the original well.
